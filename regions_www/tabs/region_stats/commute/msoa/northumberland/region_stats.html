Key information for Northumberland, MSOA geography

This document provides information about the data underlying the Propensity to Cycle Tool (PCT) for Northumberland. The data were generated on 06/07/2017 and this document was created on 2017-07-13. The PCT is an open source tool for sustainable transport planning, released under the conditions of the Affero GPL. The PCT source code can be modified by others as long as attribution is made to the original.

This version of the PCT uses origin-destination data on travel to work from the 2011 Census. The interface is currently displaying the number of commuters travelling by different modes between Middle Super Output Area zones (MSOAs, average size 3,325 commuters). There were 129,493 commuters living in Northumberland recorded in the 2011 Census. All of these commuters are included in the zones data that are visualised on the map interface and available for download.

Adding in inter-regional flows, there were 144,751 commuters living or working in Northumberland in the 2011 Census. All these commuters are included in the All flows files on the data download pages.

Straight lines, Fast routes and Quieter routes are both visualised on the map interface and available to download. These lines and routes contain all 942 flows between MSOAs that a) start or end in Northumberland, b) have a fast route distance <20km, and c) contain at least 10 commuters (by any mode, in either direction). These 942 between-zone MSOA flows contain 65,515 commuters who either live or work in Northumberland.

Between-zone MSOA flows exclude within-MSOA travel, when the MSOA zone of origin is the same as the zone of destination. Within-MSOA travel is represented by red points on the map when the lines are shown, and accounts for 22,403 commuters living in Northumberland. Between-zone flows also exclude those with no fixed place of work.

The Route Network (MSOA) is built from all between-zone MSOA fast routes that a) start and end in Northumberland, b) have a fast route distance <20km, and c) contain at least 10 commuters (by any mode, in either direction). It includes 40,228 commuters who live and work in Northumberland. The Lower Super Output Area Route Network (LSOA) is built from all between-zone LSOA fast routes that a) start or end in Northumberland, and b) have a fast route distance <20km. For more details please see User Manual C.

See Lovelace et al. (2017) for details of the methods used to estimate the cycling, health and carbon impacts of each scenario, and the methods used to visualise results at the area, line, route and route network level.


The Northumberland region

Figure 1 shows a map of Northumberland, including the regional boundary (thick black border), local authority boundaries (blue lines) and MSOA zones in the region (grey with a black border). The straight green lines represent the top 100 flows in terms of the number of commuters by any mode.

Figure 1: Map of the Northumberland region The left-hand map in Figure 2 shows the proportion of commuters in each zone with a fast route commute distance less than 10km (calculated excluding people with no fixed workplace). The average proportion was 44% across zones in Northumberland, compared with a national average of 56%. The right-hand map shows the average hilliness of the fastest routes used by commuters living in each zone. The average was 2.5% across zones in Northumberland, compared with a national average of 2.3%.

Cycling levels in each scenario for Northumberland

The proportion of commuters who cycled as their main mode of travel to work baseline (2011 Census) data in Northumberland was 1.6%, compared to the national average of 3.1% in England and Wales as a whole. The percentage of cycling expected was 2.4%, based on the distance and hilliness of commutes in the region using the baseline model for England and Wales.

Table 1 summarises the proportion of commuters walking, driving and using other modes under each scenario in Northumberland, based on aggregating across all residents in all the MSOA zones.

Health and carbon impacts in Northumberland

A modified version of the 2014 HEAT tool was used to calculate health impacts, based on the extra physical activity gained through new cycling trips in each scenario, minus the physical activity displaced in any former walking trips that have now been switched to cycling. CO2 impacts are derived from changes in distance driven per scenario.

Table 2 illustrates the health and carbon impacts of the different scenarios in Northumberland, based on aggregating across all residents in all the MSOA zones. For each scenario, comparisons are made with a counterfactual in which nobody in the region cycled ('No cyclists'), and with the observed level of cycling in the 2011 Census.
