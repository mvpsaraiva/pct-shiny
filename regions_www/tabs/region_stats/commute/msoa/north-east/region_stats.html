Key information for the North East, MSOA geography

This document provides information about the data underlying the Propensity to Cycle Tool (PCT) for the North East. The data were generated on 06/07/2017 and this document was created on 2017-07-13. The PCT is an open source tool for sustainable transport planning, released under the conditions of the Affero GPL. The PCT source code can be modified by others as long as attribution is made to the original.

This version of the PCT uses origin-destination data on travel to work from the 2011 Census. The interface is currently displaying the number of commuters travelling by different modes between Middle Super Output Area zones (MSOAs, average size 3,325 commuters). There were 934,442 commuters living in the North East recorded in the 2011 Census. All of these commuters are included in the zones data that are visualised on the map interface and available for download.

Adding in inter-regional flows, there were 968,922 commuters living or working in the North East in the 2011 Census. All these commuters are included in the All flows files on the data download pages.

Straight lines, Fast routes and Quieter routes are both visualised on the map interface and available to download. These lines and routes contain all 9,256 flows between MSOAs that a) start or end in the North East, b) have a fast route distance <20km, and c) contain at least 10 commuters (by any mode, in either direction). These 9,256 between-zone MSOA flows contain 637,393 commuters who either live or work in the North East.

Between-zone MSOA flows exclude within-MSOA travel, when the MSOA zone of origin is the same as the zone of destination. Within-MSOA travel is represented by red points on the map when the lines are shown, and accounts for 81,026 commuters living in the North East. Between-zone flows also exclude those with no fixed place of work.

The Route Network (MSOA) is built from all between-zone MSOA fast routes that a) start and end in the North East, b) have a fast route distance <20km, and c) contain at least 20 commuters (by any mode, in either direction). It includes 571,636 commuters who live and work in the North East. The Lower Super Output Area Route Network (LSOA) is built from all between-zone LSOA fast routes that a) start or end in the North East, and b) have a fast route distance <20km. For more details please see User Manual C.

See Lovelace et al. (2017) for details of the methods used to estimate the cycling, health and carbon impacts of each scenario, and the methods used to visualise results at the area, line, route and route network level.

Figure 1 shows a map of the North East, including the regional boundary (thick black border), local authority boundaries (blue lines) and MSOA zones in the region (grey with a black border). The straight green lines represent the top 100 flows in terms of the number of commuters by any mode.

Figure 1: Map of the North East region The left-hand map in Figure 2 shows the proportion of commuters in each zone with a fast route commute distance less than 10km (calculated excluding people with no fixed workplace). The average proportion was 60% across zones in the North East, compared with a national average of 56%. The right-hand map shows the average hilliness of the fastest routes used by commuters living in each zone. The average was 2.2% across zones in the North East, compared with a national average of 2.3%.

Cycling levels in each scenario for the North East

The proportion of commuters who cycled as their main mode of travel to work baseline (2011 Census) data in the North East was 1.9%, compared to the national average of 3.1% in England and Wales as a whole. The percentage of cycling expected was 3.2%, based on the distance and hilliness of commutes in the region using the baseline model for England and Wales.

Table 1 summarises the proportion of commuters walking, driving and using other modes under each scenario in the North East, based on aggregating across all residents in all the MSOA zones.

Health and carbon impacts in the North East

A modified version of the 2014 HEAT tool was used to calculate health impacts, based on the extra physical activity gained through new cycling trips in each scenario, minus the physical activity displaced in any former walking trips that have now been switched to cycling. CO2 impacts are derived from changes in distance driven per scenario.

Table 2 illustrates the health and carbon impacts of the different scenarios in the North East, based on aggregating across all residents in all the MSOA zones. For each scenario, comparisons are made with a counterfactual in which nobody in the region cycled ('No cyclists'), and with the observed level of cycling in the 2011 Census.
